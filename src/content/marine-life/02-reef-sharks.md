## Sharks at Gili Meno are good news

A reef with healthy populations of small reef sharks is, almost by definition, a healthy reef. They're an apex predator that controls the populations of mid-tier reef fish, and their presence indicates a working food web.

Around Gili Meno, the two species you'll meet most are **white-tip reef sharks** and **black-tip reef sharks**. Both are small (white-tips average 1.5 m, black-tips 1.6 m), both feed on small reef fish, and both are essentially uninterested in divers.

## Where to see them

- **[Shark Point](/dive-sites/shark-point)** — the best site for reliable encounters. White-tips rest in the overhangs during the day; black-tips patrol the deeper sand.
- **[Deep Turbo](/dive-sites/deep-turbo)** — frequent sightings around the deeper coral mounds.
- **[Meno Wall](/dive-sites/meno-wall)** — pelagic encounters along the wall, sometimes including grey reef sharks.
- **[Secret Reef](/dive-sites/secret-reef)** — occasional white-tips in the deeper overhangs.

## What you'll actually see

Reef sharks at Gili Meno typically:

- **Cruise slowly** along the bottom or just off it
- **Rest on the sand** under overhangs (white-tips do this — uniquely among sharks they can pump water over their gills without swimming)
- **Patrol in pairs or small groups** rather than schooling
- **Ignore divers** unless you make an effort to behave threateningly

You will not see hammerheads, bull sharks, tiger sharks, or great whites here. Those are different ecosystems.

## How to behave

- **Stay calm** — fast, panicked movement looks like prey behaviour.
- **Don't chase** — you won't catch them, and you'll stress an animal that's already stressed by global pressures.
- **Don't block their path** — give them open water to leave through if they want to.
- **No touching, no feeding, no flash on close-range shots.**

## Why it matters

The shark populations on the Gili reefs are a fraction of what they would have been a generation ago. Recreational diving — done with restraint — is one of the few uses of these reefs that doesn't actively reduce shark numbers, and the income it generates supports the marine protected area status that keeps illegal fishing pressure down.

In other words: dive carefully and you're part of the shark conservation story, not against it.
