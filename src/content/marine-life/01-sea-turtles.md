## Why Gili Meno is a turtle island

The shallow seagrass beds and coral plateaus around Gili Meno are exactly the kind of habitat green and hawksbill turtles need. Greens come for the seagrass — they're herbivores once mature — and hawksbills come for the sponges, algae, and small reef invertebrates.

Combine that with the protected status of the site cluster around Turtle City, the local turtle sanctuary's hatchling release programme, and the absence of motorised boats on the inner lagoon, and you get reliable turtle encounters on virtually every dive at the right sites.

## Where to see them

- **[Turtle City](/dive-sites/turtle-city)** — exactly what the name suggests. Five to a dozen turtles per dive is normal.
- **[Halik Reef](/dive-sites/halik-reef)** — frequently used by smaller juveniles
- **[Hans Reef](/dive-sites/hans-reef)** — calm, shallow, good chance of one or two on most dives
- **[Sunset Reef](/dive-sites/sunset-reef)** — late-afternoon dives often catch turtles settling for the night

## The two species you'll meet

**Green turtles (Chelonia mydas)** are the bigger, blunter-faced of the two. Adults can reach 1.5 m shell length and 150 kg. They're the ones you'll usually see grazing on seagrass beds.

**Hawksbill turtles (Eretmochelys imbricata)** are smaller, with a distinctive pointed beak and a more "scalloped" shell edge. They're more active hunters, picking through coral for sponges and small invertebrates.

Both are listed under CITES protection and both are critically endangered globally — the populations around Gili Meno are some of the better-monitored ones.

## How to watch them respectfully

- **Maintain at least one metre distance** — turtles are easily stressed by crowding.
- **Never touch or chase.** Touching removes their protective slime coating and can transmit disease. Chasing burns calories they need for surviving the journey to nesting beaches.
- **Approach below the turtle's eye line, not above.** From above looks like a predator; from below looks like terrain.
- **Don't block their path to the surface.** Turtles need to breathe; obstructing them is genuinely dangerous.
- **No flash photography on resting animals.** Wide-angle natural-light shots of behaviour are far more interesting anyway.
