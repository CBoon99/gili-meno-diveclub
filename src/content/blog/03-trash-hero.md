## Diving as conservation

Once a month, Meno Dive Club joins the local Trash Hero chapter for an underwater and beach cleanup. We take divers out to the reef and seagrass areas where rubbish tends to settle, fill mesh bags, and bring it back to the harbour to be sorted.

You don't need to be a strong diver to join. A single open-water certification is plenty, and we've taken total beginners along on the post-dive beach segment.

## Why it works

Trash Hero is a community-driven movement that started in Thailand and now has chapters across Southeast Asia. The Gili Meno chapter runs weekly beach cleanups; we add a monthly underwater dive on top.

The model is deliberately simple: show up, pick stuff up, weigh it, log it, repeat. There's no membership, no hierarchy, no fundraising pressure. Just consistent presence, week after week.

## What we find

Some of it is what you'd expect — single-use plastic, bottles, fishing line, polystyrene fragments. Some of it is heartbreaking: discarded fishing nets where turtles get tangled, cigarette butts in the sand at every popular swimming spot, microplastics threaded through seagrass roots.

We log every cleanup and share the totals on the Trash Hero Indonesia channels. The numbers go up and down with monsoons and tourism; what matters is the trend.

## What you'll do as a diver-volunteer

A typical Trash Hero dive day:

1. Briefing on the boat — site, hand signals, weight management with a partly-full bag
2. Two dives at sites known to accumulate rubbish (often the protected lagoon-side reefs)
3. Surface support and beach segment back at the dive shop
4. Sorting, weighing, and logging on the harbour
5. Lunch as a group

If you'd rather not collect rubbish underwater, you can still help on the beach side — the reef section often gets cleaned as much by surface-reach litter pickers as by divers.

## How to join

The next cleanup date is on our home page and in our newsletter. There's no charge for the dives themselves; we cover air, kit, and boat for participants. Donations to Trash Hero — for bags, gloves, beach signage — are welcome but never expected.

## Beyond cleanup days

Cleanup dives are the visible bit. The less visible — and probably more important — work is reducing waste at source. We refill water bottles for free at the dive shop, run a refundable kit-rental scheme to cut single-use packaging, and try to choose suppliers that ship in bulk rather than individual sachets.

If your accommodation, café or boat operator on Gili Meno isn't doing the same, ask why. Polite pressure from tourists is one of the most effective levers we have.

## Come dive with us

If a Trash Hero day falls during your trip, we'd love to have you. The dives themselves are gentle, the company is good, and you'll go home with a story better than "I saw some fish".
